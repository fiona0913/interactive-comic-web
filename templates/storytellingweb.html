written by
Yuqiao Ding

A masterpiece of magical realism, chronicling the century-long rise and fall of the Buendía family in the mythical town of Macondo.


Many years later, as Colonel Aureliano Buendía faced the firing squad, he would remember that distant afternoon when his father took him to discover ice.

It was a time when Macondo still slumbered beneath veils of mist. The village had no map, no direction—only the sun served as a clock to mark the passing days. The gypsies came year after year, vanishing as mysteriously as they arrived, bringing with them dreams disguised as objects: mechanical birds that sang, mirrors that claimed to reveal the soul, rods that could seize lightning.

But that year, they brought ice.

"Touch it," said José Arcadio Buendía. His eyes glowed with a rare and reckless fervor. He guided his son's hand toward the shimmering block, as if it were not frozen water, but the very edge of a miracle.

From that moment, Buendía began to dream in fever. He sketched devices, boiled strange mixtures, drifted through his days, haunted by his nights. He believed he could conquer solitude with science, fill fate's hollow with logic.

His wife, Úrsula, stood quietly at the edge of his obsessions, folding cloths and rinsing glass tubes, as if she had already accepted there would be no end to any of it.

The forests had yet to be cleared. The rain had not yet come to Macondo.

But fate had already sown its seeds. The first cycle of the Buendía family was hidden in the chill of that block of ice.

Colonel Aureliano Buendía

Colonel Aureliano Buendía

The son who remembers. Though only a child in this chapter, Aureliano's memory of seeing ice will haunt him for life, marking the first thread in his long and tragic journey.


Úrsula once dreamt of a road paved with damp earth, leading to a city she had never seen.

When she woke, she set out anyway—a small, determined woman walking through jungles, valleys, rain, and light. No one expected her to return with strangers and news from the outside world, but she did. And from then on, Macondo was no longer alone.

The children were growing.

José Arcadio, tall and silent, carried a fire behind his eyes. He met a woman like wind—she came and went in a night, and their fates were sealed. He left without goodbye, leaving only the shadow of a child behind.

Aureliano watched in silence. He spoke little but saw everything. His hands were silver-threaded and precise. Time moved around him differently, as if waiting for a prophecy to be fulfilled.

And then came Rebeca, the girl with the bag of bones. Adopted into the house, she brought no memories, only the habit of eating earth and the scent of something not yet named. She wandered the corridors at night and made the house whisper.

The family was expanding. And time began to blur at the edges.

Each believed they were writing their own story, not knowing their names were already inked into the margins of a manuscript that had long been sealed.
